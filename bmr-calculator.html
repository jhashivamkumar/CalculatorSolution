Explanation

A BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) calculator estimates how many calories your body burns each day at rest. This includes energy used for breathing, circulation, temperature regulation, and essential organ function. BMR is a helpful starting point for planning nutrition goals like weight loss, maintenance, or muscle gain.

To estimate your daily needs, many people convert BMR into TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) by applying an activity multiplier. TDEE reflects calories burned from your normal day: walking, work, exercise, and other movement. For example, if your BMR is 1,500 calories/day and your activity factor is 1.4, your TDEE estimate is about 2,100 calories/day.

Keep in mind: BMR formulas are estimates. Your actual energy needs can vary based on body composition, sleep, stress, hormonal changes, and medical conditions. Use BMR/TDEE as a baseline, then adjust based on progress over 2–3 weeks.

Tip: For sustainable results, avoid extreme calorie cuts. Aim for gradual changes and consider professional advice if you have health conditions or specific medical goals.

Formula

Common BMR equations:

Mifflin–St Jeor:
Men:    BMR = 10W + 6.25H − 5A + 5
Women:  BMR = 10W + 6.25H − 5A − 161

Harris–Benedict (revised):
Men:    BMR = 13.397W + 4.799H − 5.677A + 88.362
Women:  BMR = 9.247W + 3.098H − 4.330A + 447.593

Where:
W = weight (kg), H = height (cm), A = age (years)

To estimate daily calories (TDEE):
TDEE = BMR × ActivityFactor

FAQs

What is the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR is calories burned at rest. TDEE estimates daily calories by multiplying BMR with an activity level (movement + exercise).

Which equation should I use?

Mifflin–St Jeor is commonly used for modern estimates, while Harris–Benedict is also popular. Both provide approximations.

How accurate is this BMR calculator?

It’s an estimate. Individual factors like genetics, hormones, sleep, stress, and body composition can change real calorie needs.

Does muscle mass affect BMR?

Yes. More lean muscle generally increases BMR because muscle tissue uses more energy than fat tissue.

Does BMR decline with age?

Often yes—especially if lean muscle decreases. Strength training and adequate protein can support lean mass.
