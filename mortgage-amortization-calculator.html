What is a mortgage amortization schedule?

A mortgage amortization schedule is a table that shows how your loan balance reduces over time. Each monthly payment is split into two parts: interest (the cost of borrowing) and principal (the amount that reduces your actual loan). In the early months of a mortgage, a larger portion of your payment goes toward interest. As the balance decreases, the interest portion reduces and more of your payment goes toward principal.

This Mortgage Amortization Calculator generates a complete schedule (month-wise) so you can clearly see: your monthly payment, how much interest you pay, how quickly your balance decreases, and how extra payments can reduce total interest and shorten payoff time.

Mortgage payment formula (PMT)

Mortgage payments are calculated using the standard loan payment formula (also used for EMI). It assumes a fixed interest rate and equal payments every month. 

PMT = P × r × (1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n − 1)

Where P = loan amount, r = monthly interest rate (annual/12), and n = number of monthly payments (years × 12).

How extra payments reduce interest

When you pay extra each month, the additional amount typically goes directly to principal. This reduces your loan balance faster, which reduces future interest. Over long mortgages (like 20–30 years), small extra payments can lead to large interest savings and earlier payoff.

Mortgage Amortization Calculator FAQs

Common questions about mortgage amortization schedules, interest vs principal, and extra payments.

What is an amortization schedule?

An amortization schedule is a month-by-month table that shows your payment amount, how much goes to interest, how much goes to principal, and your remaining loan balance after each payment.

Why is the interest portion higher in the first years?

Interest is calculated on the outstanding balance. At the beginning, the balance is highest, so the interest part is larger. As your balance reduces over time, interest reduces and more of your payment goes toward principal.

How is the monthly mortgage payment (PMT/EMI) calculated?

The calculator uses the standard loan payment formula with a monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12) and number of months (years × 12). It assumes fixed interest and equal monthly payments.

What happens if I make extra payments every month?

Extra payments typically reduce the principal faster. That lowers future interest, reduces total interest paid, and shortens the payoff time. Even small extra payments can save significant interest on long-term mortgages.

Does this include property tax, insurance, or escrow?

No. This schedule covers principal + interest only. Many lenders add taxes, homeowners insurance, mortgage insurance, or escrow amounts. Add those separately to estimate your full monthly outflow.

Can the amortization schedule change over time?

Yes. If you have a floating/adjustable rate, refinance the loan, change payment frequency, or make prepayments, the schedule changes because the interest calculation and remaining balance change.

What if my interest rate is 0%?

If the rate is 0%, the monthly payment becomes principal ÷ months, and every payment goes fully toward principal (no interest).

Is this calculator accurate?

It’s a reliable estimate using standard amortization math. Final numbers may vary slightly due to lender rounding rules, payment dates, fees, escrow, and real-world amortization conventions.
