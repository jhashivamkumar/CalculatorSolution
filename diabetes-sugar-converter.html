When to Double-Check

If a number looks unusual, verify unit (mg/dL vs mmol/L) and retest using a calibrated device or lab test as advised by your clinician.

Common Mistake

People often confuse 7.0 mmol/L with 7 mg/dL. Use this converter to avoid misreading.

Explanation

Blood glucose readings are commonly reported in mg/dL (milligrams per deciliter) or mmol/L (millimoles per liter). The number may look very different depending on the unit, which can create confusion when comparing readings, checking online guidance, or sharing results with a doctor.

This converter helps you instantly switch between units using the standard glucose conversion factor (18). It’s useful for interpreting reports, tracking home glucometer readings, and avoiding unit-related mistakes—especially when traveling or reading international references.

Note: Interpretation ranges (fasting vs post-meal) depend on clinical context, the test method, and your clinician’s targets. This page provides conversion only.

Formula

mmol/L = mg/dL ÷ 18
mg/dL  = mmol/L × 18

FAQs

Why two units?

Different countries/labs use different reporting standards. US often uses mg/dL, while many others use mmol/L.

Is conversion exact?

For glucose, the standard conversion uses factor 18. (Other substances use different factors.)

Fasting vs post-meal changes the conversion?

No. Conversion stays the same. Only the clinical interpretation ranges differ.

Can I use this for HbA1c?

No. HbA1c is a different measurement with different units and conversions.

Should I act on one reading?

One reading can be affected by food, stress, illness, or device variation. Follow clinical guidance for repeat testing and diagnosis.
