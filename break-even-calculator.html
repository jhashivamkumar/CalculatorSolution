Explanation

A break-even point tells you the exact sales level where your business stops losing money and starts generating profit. It's one of the most important calculations for pricing, product launches, subscription plans, and even small shops (like cafÃ©s, stalls, and online stores). This calculator gives you break-even in units (how many items you must sell) and revenue (how much sales you need).

To calculate break-even correctly, you must separate your costs into: Fixed costs (do not change with volumeâ€”rent, salaries, subscriptions) and variable costs per unit (change per saleâ€”materials, packaging, commissions, shipping). The difference between your selling price and variable cost is the contribution margin. The bigger your contribution margin, the faster you reach break-even.

Use this page for: pricing decisions (increase price vs reduce costs), launch planning (minimum sales target), budget forecasting (monthly vs yearly break-even), and profit goal planning (units required for a specific profit target). If your selling price is less than or equal to your variable cost, you cannot break evenâ€”every sale loses money.

FAQs

What is contribution margin?

Contribution margin per unit is price minus variable cost. It pays for fixed costs first, then becomes profit.

Include all fixed costs?

Yes. Include all fixed overheads you pay during the same time period (monthly or yearly) to avoid underestimating break-even.

Multi-product business?

Use a weighted-average contribution margin based on your product mix. If your mix changes, break-even changes too.

Time horizon?

Use period-specific numbers. For example, calculate monthly break-even using monthly fixed costs, or annual break-even using annual fixed costs.

Is break-even the same as profitability?

No. Break-even is zero profit. Profit requires sales above break-even.
